For High Society, Less Recycling

The wealthiest community districts in the city are recycling less of their waste than they did a decade ago.

By Sawyer Click, Aseem Shukla and Frida Cai

Published Dec. 2018 | Repo

You might expect the Financial district at the Wall Street are filled with highly-educated people who have mastered the practice of recycling. The truth is that the rich are caring less and less about trash disposal.

A volunteer at the nonprofit county recycling organization in Montana, Britting believed that the globalization of recycling catalyzed unprecedented challenges and doubts to local systems. "Back when it first started, people were optimistic about recycling," he remarked on ABC News, "Everyone thinks they’re being recycled, but recycling is a market-driven industry.”

Poverty-struck Neighborhoods Lead Recycling Charge

More affluent neighborhood's are recycling less than they did a decade ago. But the opposite is true for less affluent neighborhoods.

Across the city, recycling is generally up. Ditsricts with high poverty levels are leading the charge.

A cluster of poverty-struck districts in the Bronx are recycling considerably more. It and Queens are the only two to have increases across the board.

But nine of the 59 districts are actually recycling less. Among these nine are the wealthiest in the city, including Manhattan's Community District 1.

The district has a median household income of $121,000, over double the city's median income. It's had the greatest percentage point decrease over the last decade in how much of it's waste it's recycling.
