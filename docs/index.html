For High Society, Less Recycling

By Sawyer Click, Aseem Shukla and Frida Cai

Published Dec. 2019 | Repo

Deep in downtown Manhattan, where skyscrapers touch the heavens and crisp suits swagger around Wall Street, the city’s wealthiest have curbed their recycling habits.

Five community districts across the city are recycling less of their waste than they were a decade ago, according to an analysis of the Department of Sanitation monthly collection reports. Among these districts are the wealthiest in the city, which the city’s targeted education campaigns have glossed over.

Poverty-stricken Districts Have Led the Charge

New York is recycling more, up from a diversion rate of 21% in 2009 to 23% in 2018, but certain districts have regressed in their recycling habits over the past decade. The slack has hindered the Department of Sanitation's dreams of contributing zero waste by 2030.

The city's recycling push has been years in the making, largely focusing on communities that had historically low diversion rates, which mostly translated to less affluent districts.

The push was fueled by the poverty-stricken districts that recycled considerably more, particularly in the Bronx.

**High poverty rate defined as greater than 15%

The Department of Sanitation's concerted education efforts with neighborhood institutions in the last decade helped bridge cultural barriers, such as language, in these areas, according to Espinoza.

But when it came to districts that are recycling less, the five culprits were spread across lower Manhattan, Staten Island and Brooklyn.

The two chart-topping districts are among the high-wealth cluster in lower Manhattan and northweste Brooklyn.

**High income defined as a district having a median household income of greater than $90,000. The city's median is nearly $58,000, per the Census.

These Manhattan districts, CD 1 and CD 6, faced the starkest drops.

The greatest fall was in the elite Community District 1, which contains Wall Street, Tribeca and Battery Park City. The district's diversion rate fell six percentage points, from 53% in 2009 to 47% in 2018.

It's also tied for the wealthiest in the city with a median household income of $121,000, more than double the city's median of nearly $58,000.

But sociologists, environmental advocates and city officials are stumped as to why these five districts have reported decreases and offered no speculation. But Adriana Espinoza, New York City Program Director for the New York League of Conservation Voters, said it comes down to the fact that officials haven't placed enough resources into Zero Waste, the city's initiative to contribute no waste to landfills by 2030.

So far, most of DSNY's education campaigns have targeted specific communities, especially immigrant and poverty-stricken communities where cultural and language barriers anguished efforts for years. But in order to reach Zero Waste's goal, Espinoza said the city would have to put in money, time and resources akin to VisionZero, the city's popular intiative to prevent traffic accidents and fatalities that has boasted impressive numbers.

"Our point that we try to make is that we don't think that that's enough," Espinoza said. "We'd like to see Zero Waste education on that same level."

Public bins and foreign borns

Foreign-born residents have less access to public recycling resources, compared to native citizens. The redder, the higher percentage of residents of the borough who were not U.S. citizens at birth. This includes naturalized U.S. citizens, lawful permanent residents, temporary migrants, humanitarian migrants, and unauthorized migrants.

A Waste Bump Threatens The Zero Waste Progress

But New York’s waste problem runs deeper than recycling; piles of garbage line are known to line streets all throughout the boroughs, leaving a signature stench. A 2015 study found there's good reason for the both of these: New York is the most wasteful city in the world.

The city emits more trash than any other megacity in the world, according to a 2015 study that detailed energy and material flows of 27 megacities. The study found that the city that never sleeps produced about 34 megatons of waste in 2011, more than three times the second place Mexico City.

The city has made meaningful gains in its recycling habits, but it’s still just a sliver when compared to the non-recycled waste. And a bump in waste emission in 2018 threatens what recycling gains have been made, indicating a slowdown for the first time in recent years.
