For High Society, Less Recycling

By Sawyer Click, Aseem Shukla and Frida Cai

Published Dec. 2018 | Repo

Deep in downtown Manhattan, where skyscrapers touch the heavens and crisp suits swagger around Wall Street, the city’s wealthiest have curbed their recycling habits.

Five community districts across the city are recycling less of their waste than they were a decade ago, according to an analysis of the Department of Sanitation monthly collection reports. Among these districts are the wealthiest in the city, which the city’s targeted education campaigns glossed over.

Department of Sanitation officials, sociologists, and enviornmental advocates are stumped as to why these five districts have reported decreases. But Adriana Espinoza, New York City Program Director at the New York League of Conservation Voters, said that

Poverty-stricken Districts Lead the Charge

They city is doing better as a whole, but certain districts have regressed in their recycling habits, hindering the the Department of Sanitation's ZeroWaste dreams.

The city as a whole has made a slight gain in its diversion rate, up from 21% of trash recycled in 2009 to 23% in 2018.

The push was fueled by the poverty-stricken districts that recycled considerably more, particularly in the Bronx.

**High poverty rate defined as greater than 15%

The Department of Sanitation's concerted education efforts with neighborhood institutions in the last decade helped bridge cultural barriers, such as language, in these areas, according to Espinoza.

But when it came to districts that are recycling less, the five culprits were spread across lower Manhattan, Staten Island and Brooklyn.

The two chart-topping districts were among the cluster of wealth-concentrated cluster in lower Manhattan and northwestern Brooklyn.

**High income defined as a district having a median household income of greater than $90,000. The city's median is nearly $58,000, per the Census.

These Manhattan districts, CD 1 and CD 6, faced the starkest drops.

The greatest fall was in the elite Community District 1, which contains Wall Street, Tribeca and Battery Park City. The district's recycling fell six percentage points, from 53% in 2009 to 47% in 2018.

The district is tied for the wealthiest in the city with a median household income of $121,000, more than double the city's median of nearly $58,000.
